Data brief · OECD climate finance

Where climate money flows — India and six peers

A working summary of public climate aid and privately mobilised climate finance across seven large, climate-exposed developing economies, 2015–2024. Built to surface trends, not to settle them.

Peers: India · China · Brazil · Indonesia · Bangladesh · Pakistan · Viet Nam  |  Source: OECD DAC (Rio Markers; CRS private mobilisation)

Why these seven

The comparison set

India is hard to benchmark: it is a lower-middle-income country by GNI per capita, yet carries the economy, emissions, and negotiating weight of an upper-middle-income G20 power. So peers were chosen on two axes at once — economic scale and political grouping (the G20 / BASIC bloc: China, Brazil, Indonesia, South Africa) and climate vulnerability and need (Bangladesh, Pakistan, Viet Nam, Indonesia). The seven that carry through the finance data are India, China, Brazil, Indonesia, Bangladesh, Pakistan and Viet Nam.

Vulnerability framing matters because it sets up the central tension in the finance numbers. Across three independent indices, India is an outlier on physical risk relative to its income peers:

Part one · Public money

Climate aid committed by DAC donors

The first dataset is public ODA committed to climate, tagged through the OECD's Rio Markers, in constant 2024 dollars. India dominates this picture — it alone accounts for roughly 42% of all climate finance committed to the seven, driven overwhelmingly by mitigation.

The balance between mitigation and adaptation is where the structural story sits. Donors fund India's clean-energy and transport agenda far more than its adaptation needs — a 3.3-to-1 tilt — despite India ranking second globally on physical climate risk. Pakistan is the mirror image: the only peer where adaptation finance exceeds mitigation.

Over time, a clear divergence opened after 2020. The South and Southeast Asian frontline economies saw large increases — India, Bangladesh, Indonesia and Pakistan all rose, with adaptation finance roughly tripling for Indonesia and Bangladesh in the back half of the decade. China, Viet Nam and Pakistan's mitigation lines fell. China's decline is the sharpest, reflecting its graduation away from being a DAC aid priority.

Public finance — what stands out

  • India absorbs ~42% of the group's climate ODA; Bangladesh (19%) and Indonesia (16%) form the second tier.
  • Mitigation crowds out adaptation for the large economies. India's 3.3:1 tilt is striking given it is the most physically exposed peer.
  • Adaptation is rising fastest post-2020 across South/SE Asia — tripling for Indonesia and Bangladesh — hinting at a slow correction.
  • China is exiting the DAC aid frame: climate, biodiversity (−80%) and environment finance all fell sharply.
  • Quality differs: India and Brazil have the highest "Principal" shares (genuinely climate-purposed projects); adaptation is far more often a side-benefit than a main objective everywhere.
Part two · Private money

Private finance mobilised by multilaterals

The second dataset measures something different: private capital that multilateral institutions (World Bank/IFC, ADB and peers) pull into climate projects by de-risking deals. This reorders the league table entirely. On private mobilisation, Brazil leads — not India — mobilising about 1.7× what India did.

The instruments — the "tools" — reveal each market's maturity. Across all six, mobilisation concentrates in market-based vehicles: direct equity investment in companies and SPVs (45% of the total), syndicated loans (23%), guarantees (13%) and shares in collective investment vehicles (13%). But the mix per country differs sharply.

The trend over time is one of concentration, not democratisation. Brazil's mobilisation exploded (+677% between the two halves of the decade); India more than doubled. But Pakistan collapsed (−61%) and China declined — the gap between bankable and fragile markets is widening.

Private finance — what stands out

  • Brazil, not India, leads private mobilisation ($17bn vs $10bn). The ODA ranking inverts because private capital chases bankability.
  • The toolkit signals maturity: deep markets get equity + syndicated loans; fragile markets need guarantees first.
  • India is the CIV outlier — pooled fund vehicles do its heavy lifting, more than any peer.
  • Multilaterals dominate the channel — 82–91% of official private mobilisation for Brazil, China and Viet Nam. Bilateral DAC donors are minor by comparison (Pakistan is the lone exception).
  • Concentration is intensifying: Brazil +677%, India +121%, but Pakistan −61% and China −30%. The most vulnerable mobilise the least.
The thesis

The gap between need and capital

Put the two halves together and a single pattern emerges. Public climate aid roughly follows scale and, increasingly, need — India absorbs the most, and adaptation is finally rising for the vulnerable South Asian economies. But private climate capital flows to where it is safe, not where it is needed. The countries that scored highest on physical climate risk — Bangladesh, Pakistan, and India on the hazard-exposure measures — are precisely the ones mobilising the least private money, and the gap is growing.

Inferences for the research direction

  • The adaptation finance gap is the sharpest story. India's 3.3:1 mitigation tilt against its #2 global risk ranking is a quantifiable injustice worth a dedicated chapter.
  • "Mobilisation follows bankability" is testable. The Brazil-vs-India inversion between public and private finance is a clean natural experiment in what de-risking can and cannot reach.
  • Instruments encode market maturity. The guarantee-heavy mix in Pakistan/Bangladesh vs equity in Brazil is a measurable proxy — a possible index of "blended-finance readiness."
  • China's exit from both public aid and (declining) private mobilisation makes it a useful control rather than a peer — worth reclassifying.
  • Commitments ≠ disbursements, and markers overlap. Any headline number needs this caveat; a disbursement-based robustness check would strengthen the work.
  • Per-capita and per-emissions normalisation is the obvious next cut — absolute totals flatter India and Brazil simply because they are large.

Two caveats carried throughout: all figures are commitments / amounts mobilised, not disbursements (actual money flowing can differ and lags), and the Rio markers overlap (a project can be tagged for several objectives, so markers cannot simply be summed). Indonesia appears in the public-finance data but not the private-mobilisation file.
